Risk management: how we try to make the ride
survivable?
We can’t remove volatility, but we can control exposure and quality. We cap single-name exposure, avoid leverage-heavy balance sheets and
opaque governance,and follow an explicit sell discipline so that risk is acted on, not discussed in hindsight.​
Played out: the thesis or valuation has run its course → we exit or trim.​
When a stock moves from “undervalued with triggers ahead” to “fully valued with limited upside,” we start taking money off the table.​
Example: if we buy a niche engineering company at 12–13x earnings with a strong orderbook, and over 18–24 months earnings double and the stock re-rates to
28–30x, we typically trim or exit even if the business remains goodthe upside from there rarely compensates for the downside if anything goes wrong.​
Broken: governance or balance sheet deteriorates → we exit quickly.​
We track auditor changes, pledging, working-capital stretch and related-party transactions. If the facts change, we don’t “average down,” we walk away.​
Example: in the past we have exited a mid-cap where receivable days spiked and management started guiding aggressively while cash flows lagged; the stock
fellfurther later, but our capital was already protected.​
Better use of capital: a higher-IRR idea emerges → we recycle.​
Even a decent holding can become a “source of funds” if we find a clearly superior risk-reward elsewhere.​
Example: we may move capital from a steady consumer business compounding at 12–15% annually into a manufacturing/exports name where capacity is coming
onstream and we expect 20%+ earnings CAGR over the next 3–5 years with similar downside risk.​
Risk control: position, sector or liquidity looks stretched → we reduce.​
We keep hard checks on single-stock weight, sector concentration and daily liquidity. If any of these breach our internal bands, we cut exposure irrespective of
how much we “like” the story.​
Example: if a stock has rallied sharply and grows to 8–10% of the portfolio or if a sector (say capital goods or financials) crosses our comfort band, we trim it
back to size and redeploy into under-owned, high-quality names, keeping the portfolio balanced.

Govt thrust creating sectoral opportunities
Defense Modernization

India's defense sector is undergoing significant modernization, with orders worth ₹2,00,000 crore
expected by 2030. The "Atmanirbhar Bharat" initiative drives self reliance, allocating 68% of the FY24
defense budget to domestic procurement. Defense exports are targeted to reach ₹41,500 crore by 2025,
up from ₹12,450 crore in FY23. The sector presents robust growth in manufacturing and R&D, aiming to
establish a strong indigenous defense ecosystem.

Railway Expansion

The National Rail Plan envisions expanding railway capacity by 2030 to support growth projections until
2050. Freight modal share is set to rise from 26% to 45%, while high speed rail projects, such as the
Mumbai-Ahmedabad bullet train, will transform passenger travel. With a record INR 2.4 lakh crore capex
allocation for FY24, India targets becoming a global leader in railway infrastructure, aiming for a 100%
electrified broad gauge network by 2030.

Renewable Energy

India targets achieving 500 GW of nonfossil fuel based energy capacity by 2030, aligning with its
commitments under the Paris Agreement. Policy initiatives like the National Green Hydrogen Mission and
Offshore Wind Energy targets are driving investments worth INR 20 lakh crore into solar, wind, and
hydroelectric power. The government’s aim is to reduce carbon intensity by 45% and achieve net zero by
2070.

Biofuel Industry

India's biofuel sector is set to achieve a 20% ethanol blending target by 2025 and 25% by 2030 under the
National BioEnergy Mission. The government is investing INR 35,000 crore in biorefineries and feedstock
technologies. By 2030, the biofuel industry is expected to reduce India’s crude oil imports by 15% and cut
greenhouse gas emissions by 30%, bolstering the country’s energy security and sustainability goals.

Shipbuilding Industry

The Maritime India Vision 2030 targets India’s rise as a global shipbuilding leader, increasing gross tonnage
to 500k GT and tripling the ship repair industry to ₹12,000-₹15,000 crore. Green shipping initiatives,
including hybrid propulsion incentives and hydrogen retrofitting, are key drivers. Indian shipyards foresee
₹2,80,000 crore in opportunities over 15 years, with ₹1,80,000 crore from defense and ₹100,000 crore from
commercial shipbuilding. The ship repair market is set to grow to ₹16,000-₹20,000 crore annually by 2030,
supported by fleet upgrades and conversions.

Fulcrum Fees: Transparent & Aligned
Our Fee Structure
Fixed Fee: 1.5% of portfolio value
Fulcrum Fee: 25% on returns exceeding the benchmark (Alpha)

How It Works
Benchmark: BSE 500 Total Return Index (TRI) with 10% return
Portfolio Performance: 15%
Excess Return (Alpha): 15% - 10% = 5%
Fulcrum Fee: 25% of 5% = 1.25%
Total Fee Charged: 1.5% Fixed + 1.25% Fulcrum = 2.75%

Why This Benefits Clients
Fair & Aligned: Pay only for outperformance beyond the benchmark.
Incentive for Alpha: Encourages managers to focus on maximizing client gains.
Transparent & Simple: No complex hurdle rates or hidden charges.

Fee Structure
Fixed Fees: Incurred on a semi annual basis.
Fulcrum Fees: Levied annually at the end of the year. In the event of a redemption within the year, the fee will be adjusted
proportionally based on the holding period.
